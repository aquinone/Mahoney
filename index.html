“He’s lived more lives than a cat,” Upper School Math teacher Chris van Benthuysen said, describing the longest-serving member of his department, Ed Mahoney.

Indeed, though this year is his 22nd year here, Mr. Mahoney’s path to Latin spans copious continents and careers—not a linear road at all. “I kind of stumbled into teaching,” he said. “I didn’t know that that’s what I wanted to do, ever.”

Along the way, he worked in construction, finance, restaurants, and photography. He traveled to refugee camps, ship yards, forests, and Japanese ports. Every story builds the same awed sense among those who know him.

“Every time some random story about something pops up that’s unrelated, he’ll be like ‘oh, yeah, I did that for six months,’” Mr. van Benthuysen said.

This long list of experience adds to his teaching immensely, allowing him to empathize with many perspectives. Mr. van Benthuysen said, “He just has seen the world through so many different lenses, that it’s an invaluable asset to have within the department.”

For his students, this real-world focus helps put skills in context. One former student and teaching assistant, Michael Cardoza ‘24, spoke to Mr. Mahoney’s unique classroom style.

“For the most part, class is completely project based,” Michael said. “While there is conceptual (and even historical) learning, most of the actual education comes in the form of concept application. Not only that, but the application is wildly practical.”

Mr. van Benthuysen sees Mr. Mahoney’s insight present itself in diverse discussions regularly. “Lots of members of my department—when we’re in conversation about something, debating something philosophical or something like that—will offer a perspective that’s one I had not considered and it will open my eyes,” he said. “He’s the one that does that more often than anybody else in the department.”
